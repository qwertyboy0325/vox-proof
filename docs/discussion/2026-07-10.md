## 1. Context for an external reader

VoxProof is a local-first, evidence-backed transcript QA tool. Its v0.1 scope is post-ASR review, not ASR generation: it takes an existing SRT subtitle file, surfaces candidate errors as review cases backed by evidence (currently a glossary-based detector), records human accept/reject/defer decisions in an append-only ledger, and derives a reviewed SRT plus a decision log. No transcript text is ever silently rewritten; human decisions are canonical.

State as of this discussion:

- The full v0.1 code loop is implemented and runnable locally as a CLI: parse SRT, detect glossary candidates, review interactively, derive reviewed SRT, write decision log. It has passed only synthetic smoke tests.
- The implementation finished faster than planned. Real-material validation has not happened.
- The primary target user is "Lead A", a YouTube subtitle editor with reported pain around misrecognized proper nouns in ASR drafts. Groundwork for testing with Lead A has been laid, but the working assumption is that no third-party tester is available yet.

The question of this discussion: given no third-party tester, how should product pacing be adjusted, and what non-blocking progress and acceptance standards should replace "wait for a real user"?

## 2. Owner decisions (binding direction)

These positions were set by the project owner during the discussion:

1. Requiring a third person to judge success or failure of the current validation target is unrealistic and overly conservative. The reviewer's initial position, which gated v0.1 judgment on third-party involvement, was rejected.
2. The product sequence is: build an MVP against the hypothesized pain point, then give it to the real target cohort — the people with the pain — to test.
3. There will be one consolidated external test with the third party, not per-slice experiments. Preparation must be sufficient to convince them to accept the test.
4. Progress and acceptance standards for the current phase must be non-blocking: completable and judgeable without waiting on any third party.

## 3. Reviewer reasoning offered in response (accepted framing, not criteria)

After the owner's pushback in point 1 above, the reviewer offered this reasoning: "is v0.1 successful" conflates two independent questions.

1. Mechanism validity: on real material, does the review flow catch real errors, at what recall and precision, and how long does a pass take? These are measurable facts. The judge is data, not a person. A third party's only unique contribution here is material, and material can come from self-owned or authorized real content instead.
2. Adoption willingness: does someone with the pain want this? This genuinely requires the target cohort, and is deferred to the eventual Lead A session. Self-testing is not claimed to answer it.

## 4. Reviewer proposals (non-binding, not yet decided by the owner)

Everything in this section originates from the assistant reviewer. The owner has seen these proposals but has not confirmed, adjusted, or rejected them item by item. None of it is a decision, a gate, or a freeze. It is input for the next round of discussion.

### 4.1 Proposed self-material pilot procedure

1. Obtain one real ASR draft of self-owned or authorized content, produced outside the product. Prefer material that predates the plan, to avoid unconsciously selecting material the glossary can catch.
2. Hand-correct the draft to a final version. The diff is the ground truth: every real error categorized (enumerable term / homophone requiring context / colloquial rephrasing / segmentation / timing / other), each also marked "would I have made this correction anyway" to separate real fixes from cosmetic ones.
3. Pre-build a glossary for the material, run the full review flow, measure against ground truth.

### 4.2 Proposed pass/fail thresholds

Proposed pass indicators: end-to-end run completes on real material; detector recall on the enumerable-term class at least 80% (distinguishing glossary-coverage gaps from mechanism failures); precision of surfaced cases at least 60%; per-decision handling time recorded as a baseline.

Proposed failure indicators: enumerable-term errors under 20% of all real errors (glossary-driven interception ceiling too low); or the matcher missing most correctly listed targets.

The 80% / 60% / 20% numbers are reviewer-proposed defaults only. The owner has not confirmed them.

### 4.3 Proposed labeling rule

A passing self-test result would be stated as "the mechanism works on real material", nothing more — not product validation, not evidence of demand.

### 4.4 Proposed third-party test readiness checklist

1. Mechanism proven on real material.
2. A one-page pilot result to show.
3. Lead A's cost stated in one sentence, with a measured (not guessed) session length.
4. Full session rehearsed end to end at least once.
5. Lead A's glossary pre-built from their public content.
6. Data-handling statement ready.

### 4.5 Proposed code posture during the phase

Prioritize pilot work over new product slices; fix pilot-discovered defects test-first; swap demo glossary content without building a glossary file format. This was proposed as a freeze; it is not one, because the owner has not accepted it.

## 5. Open items for the next discussion

- Whether the mechanism-validity criteria in 4.2 (procedure, thresholds) are the right acceptance standard, and at what values.
- Whether the readiness checklist in 4.4 is the right definition of "sufficiently prepared", and what its items should be.
- What code work, if any, is in scope during this phase.
