# VoxProof Pacing Adjustment and Validation Gates Discussion

Status: discussion / non-binding

Source: project owner and assistant reviewer discussion

Date: 2026-07-10

Owns:
A self-contained record of the 2026-07-10 pacing discussion and its reasoning, written so a third party (human or LLM) without repository access can follow it.

Does not own:
The canonical criteria themselves. The binding versions of the Track 2 pass/fail criteria and the third-party test readiness checklist live in `docs/product/v0.1-execution-order.md`. If this record and that document disagree, that document wins.

Rule:
Nothing in this document authorizes implementation.

## 1. Context for an external reader

VoxProof is a local-first, evidence-backed transcript QA tool. Its v0.1 scope is post-ASR review, not ASR generation: it takes an existing SRT subtitle file, surfaces candidate errors as review cases backed by evidence (currently a glossary-based detector), records human accept/reject/defer decisions in an append-only ledger, and derives a reviewed SRT plus a decision log. No transcript text is ever silently rewritten; human decisions are canonical.

State as of this discussion:

- The full v0.1 code loop is implemented and runnable locally as a CLI: parse SRT, detect glossary candidates, review interactively, derive reviewed SRT, write decision log. It has passed only synthetic smoke tests.
- The implementation finished faster than planned. Real-material validation has not happened.
- The primary target user is "Lead A", a YouTube subtitle editor with reported pain around misrecognized proper nouns in ASR drafts. Groundwork for testing with Lead A has been laid, but the working assumption is that no third-party tester is available yet.

The question of this discussion: given no third-party tester, how should product pacing be adjusted, and what non-blocking progress and acceptance standards should replace "wait for a real user"?

## 2. Rejected position: third-party judgment as the validation gate

The initial reviewer position made third-party involvement the bar for judging v0.1 success. The project owner pushed back: requiring a third person to judge success or failure is unrealistic and overly conservative.

The pushback was accepted as correct, on this reasoning: "is v0.1 successful" conflates two independent questions.

1. Mechanism validity: on real material, does the review flow catch real errors, at what recall and precision, and how long does a pass take? These are measurable facts. The judge is data, not a person. A third party's only unique contribution here is material, and material can come from self-owned or authorized real content instead.
2. Adoption willingness: does someone with the pain want this? This genuinely requires the target cohort, and is deferred to the eventual Lead A session. Self-testing must never be claimed to answer it.

Gating question 1 on a third party lets an objectively measurable question be blocked by an uncontrollable external dependency. That was the error.

## 3. Confirmed product sequence

The owner's framing was confirmed as the standard sequence: build an MVP against the hypothesized pain point, then give it to the real target cohort — the people with the pain — to test. Cohort testing validates pain and value; it is the second gate, not the first. The first gate (mechanism validity) must pass before the MVP is worth putting in front of the cohort at all, because a mechanically weak demo burns the one first impression available with Lead A.

## 4. One consolidated external test, not per-slice experiments

The owner set a further constraint: do not run each small implementation slice as a separate experiment with third parties. Preparation must be sufficient to convince them to accept one test.

Agreed structure: internal work continues in small bounded slices (engineering discipline unchanged), but externally there is exactly one consolidated test event. All slices aggregate toward it.

To keep "prepared enough" from becoming indefinite polishing, readiness is defined by a closed checklist — all items checked means schedule the session, any unchecked means not yet:

1. Mechanism proven (Track 2 pass criteria met on real material).
2. One-page pilot result to show ("on one real ASR draft, the flow surfaced M of N enumerable-term errors").
3. Lead A's cost stated in one sentence: one or two episodes' draft plus final subtitles, and one session of a measured (not guessed) length.
4. Full session rehearsed end to end at least once; script, record sheet, and metrics final.
5. Lead A's glossary pre-built from their public content, so the session shows their errors caught.
6. Data-handling statement ready (usage, storage, nothing leaves the machine).

## 5. Track 2: self-judged mechanism validation (the new current phase)

Pilot procedure, requiring no third party and no new product code:

1. Obtain one real ASR draft of self-owned or authorized content, produced outside the product. The material must predate the plan; nothing is recorded for the test.
2. Hand-correct the draft to a final version. The diff is the ground truth: every real error categorized (enumerable term / homophone requiring context / colloquial rephrasing / segmentation / timing / other), each also marked "would I have made this correction anyway" to separate real fixes from cosmetic ones.
3. Pre-build a glossary for the material, run the full review flow, measure against ground truth.

Pass criteria (all required): end-to-end run completes on real material; detector recall on the enumerable-term class is at least 80% (a miss from a term absent in the glossary is a coverage gap, not a mechanism failure; a miss on a correctly listed term is a mechanism failure); precision of surfaced cases is at least 60%; per-decision handling time is recorded as a baseline.

Failure criteria (any one triggers direction review before further feature work): enumerable-term errors are under 20% of all real errors, meaning the glossary-driven interception ceiling is too low; or the matcher misses most correctly listed targets.

The 80% / 60% / 20% thresholds are agreed defaults, adjustable only with an explicit note in the canonical document.

Labeling rule: a passing result is stated as "the mechanism works on real material", nothing more. It is not product validation and not evidence of demand.

Known self-testing biases and their guards: the tester knows the glossary contents, so material must predate the plan rather than being produced for it; the tester may count cosmetic corrections as wins, hence the "would I have made this correction anyway" column in the ground truth.

## 6. Code posture during Track 2

No new product slices. Permitted changes only: defects found by the pilot (reproduced by a test before fixing) and swapping the built-in demo glossary content (no glossary file format, no config system). Findings that suggest new detector capabilities are recorded in discussion documents; building them is a decision for after Lead A material arrives.
