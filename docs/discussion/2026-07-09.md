## 1. Concrete leads

Three concrete leads exist. They are leads, not validated demand. Talking to them and observing real material is the validation.

### Lead A: high-frequency YouTube content editor

A working editor for a travel/lifestyle YouTube channel who handles subtitles, transcripts, and ASR drafts.

Reported pain surface:

- place names, shop names, person names, and brand names wrong in ASR drafts
- mixed Chinese-English recognition errors
- colloquial speech misrecognized
- repeated manual subtitle correction passes

Signal strength: this lead has expressed concrete willingness to try the tool, and the pain is directly tied to their paid working hours. This is currently the strongest lead on willingness.

### Lead B: film subtitle corrector using AI-assisted correction

A subtitle worker who already uses AI to assist film subtitle correction.

Reported pain surface:

- AI helps with text correction, but subtitle timing frequently drifts
- corrected subtitles can fall out of sync with picture and speech
- humans still re-check timecodes after AI passes
- correct text alone does not make a subtitle deliverable
- subtitle QA spans text, context, timing, and alignment quality

Product significance: this lead reframes the need from transcript correction toward subtitle QA. Text correctness is necessary but not sufficient for deliverability.

### Lead C: teacher organizing lecture content

A teacher who records lessons, wants transcripts of teaching content, corrects errors, and reuses the corrected material for review and summarization.

Product significance: recurring course terminology across sessions is a natural test of the Language Pack reuse hypothesis.

## 3. Prioritization

Two orderings matter and they disagree:

- By information value for product direction: Lead B first, because subtitle timing QA challenges the current text-first model the most.
- By willingness and validation cost: Lead A first, because willingness is expressed, the pain is tied to paid work, and authorized fixture material is most plausible from their own content.

Working resolution: pursue Lead A first for hands-on validation and fixture acquisition, use Lead B primarily as a semantic-input interview (film subtitle content is unlikely to be usable as fixture material for rights reasons), and treat Lead C as the Language Pack reuse probe.

Fixture rights expectation: Lead A > Lead C > Lead B.

## 4. Hypothesis mapping

- Lead A maps to the mixed Chinese-English technical content hypothesis.
- Lead B maps to the cross-ASR post-processing hypothesis.
- Lead C maps to the Language Pack reuse hypothesis.

Validation sketches:

- Lead A: obtain or observe one episode's ASR draft versus final subtitles; measure error density and error-type distribution. If proper-noun errors are rare per episode, the glossary wedge weakens.
- Lead B: ask how time splits between text fixing and timecode checking in the current AI-assisted flow, and which failures force a full re-watch.
- Lead C: compare transcripts of consecutive sessions of the same course; measure how much of the error surface repeats.

## 5. Model observation: segment-scoped timing findings

Lead B exposes a concrete expressiveness gap, recorded here for future design and not for current implementation:

- A timing finding ("this cue starts too early / runs too long") is scoped to a whole segment, not to a text span.
- The current `SourceAnchor` requires a non-empty byte range over one segment's text, so a segment-scoped timing finding has no natural anchor today.
- `start_ms` / `end_ms` already participate in the transcript revision identity (MD-001), so timing corrections would correctly produce new revisions; the identity layer needs no change.
- The existing `DetectionKind` rule already requires a real uncovered review case with understood localized review semantics before a new category is added; Lead B is a candidate source for exactly that understanding.

Wrong move to avoid: jumping into audio decoding or forced alignment because Lead B is persuasive. Audio-backed timing detection remains out of scope; the near-term use of Lead B is interviewing and semantics, not implementation.
